Mood and Modality  

TUTORIAL

Fundamentals:
Modal Verbs

Review modal verbs in the Practice Book.

Mood refers to our intention when we communicate a message. For example, we often make statements (declarative or indicative mood) and ask questions (interrogative mood). Other moods are:

Mood Use to Example
imperative base verb (+ object) make commands and give orders Holding her torch aloft, liberty is pointing the way onward and upward and saying to America, “Come.”
subjunctive subject + base verb express wishes, suggestions, desires Sometimes, I wish I were old enough to vote.
I suggest that you be patient.
conditional if/when + subject + verb, + result clause describe the result of a true or untrue condition If parties prefer to postpone action longer and thus do battle with this idea, they challenge the inevitable.

Modality refers to a writer or speaker’s attitude. We use modal verbs (not) + base form of a main verb to express attitudes such as: certainty (He will go.), possibility (I may/might/could go.), obligation or necessity (We must go.), advisability (You should go.), ability (I can go.).

Note: The modal verbs would, might, and could are often used in a result clause in the conditional mood.

If I had a car, I would drive it every day.

Don’t give up . . . C’mon, have a bite.

Tip!

Grammar

The subjunctive mood is used more often in very formal situations. It is not that common in English.
I recommend that you be on time. (formal, subjunctive)
You should be on time. (less formal, modal)

    C

    Choose the modal that best completes the sentence.

    1. What _______ I do: start my essay or go to the party?
      (may, should)
    2. If I won $10,000, I _______ buy a nice gift for my mother.
      (will, would)
    3. It’s set. Our class _______ have a fundraiser on April 5.
      (will, would)
    4. _______ you give a good speech if you had to?
      (may, could)
    5. You _______ start your essay the night before it’s due.
      (shouldn’t, can’t)
    6. He _______ have to work. He’s waiting to hear from his boss.
      (would, might)
