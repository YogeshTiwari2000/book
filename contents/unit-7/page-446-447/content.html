ENGAGE WITH LISTENING AND SPEAKING

Listen Debate

A debate is an organized argument. Two speakers with opposing viewpoints each take a turn presenting their side of the argument.

The speakers use rhetoric to persuade the audience to agree with their viewpoint.

The audience listens to both sides of the debate and chooses a side to agree with. The side that communicates their ideas more effectively usually wins the debate.

LISTENING STRATEGY
Use Appeals to Logic, Credibility, and Emotion

As you listen to the two sides of the argument, make note of how each speaker appeals to the following:

  • logic: reasons or facts
  • credibility: the speaker's expertise
  • emotion: your feelings about the topic
A
Listen to the debate, “Is the Future of School Online?”
B
Discuss the questions.
  1. What is Speaker A's viewpoint? What is Speaker B's viewpoint?
  2. What was an appeal to logic that you heard?
  3. What was an appeal to emotion that you heard?
  4. What was an appeal to credibility that you heard?
  5. Which side communicated their ideas more effectively and explained their rationale more persuasively? Why do you think so?
  6. Which viewpoint do you agree with most? Why?

Present Debate

SPEAKING STRATEGY
Use Appeals to Logic, Credibility, and Emotion

To speak persuasively, include the following in your argument: appeals to logic (facts, statistics), appeals to emotion (descriptive language and sensory details), and appeals to credibility (reasons and evidence that the audience can trust your viewpoint).

You listened to a debate about the future of school. Now you will research, write, and present a debate on a topic that you choose.

A
Discuss and make a list of debate topics that are of interest to you.
B
Plan your debate.
  • Identify the two sides of the debate.
  • Determine who will represent each side.
  • Choose three important points that will persuade listeners to agree with your point of view.
  • Support your points with evidence and persuasive techniques.
  • Practice your debate.
  • Record your debate.
C
Listen to your debate. Score your performance according to the rubric.

Oral Presentation Rubric

Content & Organization

  • clearly states poin of view
  • makes three points to support argument
  • appeals to logic, , emotion, and credibility

Language Conventions

  • few to no grammatical errors
  • meaning is clear

Vocabulary

  • uses academic vocabulary
  • expresses tone
  • demonstrates rhetoric

Presentation & Delivery

  • includes only intentional pauses
  • free of pronunciation errors that interfere with understanding
