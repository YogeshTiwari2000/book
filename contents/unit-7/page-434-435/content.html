Genre Argument (Persuasive Speech)

You will read the persuasive speech “Address to Congress on Women’s Suffrage” by Carrie Chapman Catt. A persuasive speech is an oral argument. Catt attempts to persuade her listeners to agree with her point of view.

Reading Strategy Synthesize

Synthesizing information can deepen your understanding of a text. When you synthesize, you pull together all of the information you have and combine it to create a new understanding. In order to synthesize information:

  • Use prior knowledge: What do you already know about this topic? How can you combine it with new information?
  • Make connections: How does this information connect to other texts you have read or experiences you have had?
  • Combine information: How can you combine all this information into new ideas?

Map It! Text Structure: Argument

An argument begins with a claim that states an opinion or viewpoint. To persuade the reader, the author provides a rationale for the claim (usually several reasons) and supports them with evidence. Transitional words and phrases, such as first of all, second, finally, help to organize the reasons.

An argument also addresses an opposing point of view, or a counterclaim, and gives evidence to persuade the reader against this idea. Evidence that explains why the counterclaim is incorrect is called a rebuttal. Transitional words and phrases, such as but, however, while, indicate the rebuttal’s contrasting ideas.

You can use a graphic organizer to help keep track of the parts of an argument while reading.

The Case for Going to School

1

“Please let me go to school!” At one time, that would have been an unusual statement from a teenager, but remote learning has changed us. We now understand that in-person learning is much more effective and enjoyable than remote learning.

2

First of all, remote learning is more difficult than in-person learning. For example, it is harder to hear and understand a teacher through a computer screen. If they use diagrams or pictures on the board, it is harder to see them. And, just imagine when they are introducing a new math topic. Forget about it!

3

Second, it is difficult to engage with learning when you cannot interact with your teacher and other students. Class discussion and participation are important elements of learning that are missing from remote learning.

4

Finally, remote learning is just not as fun. Seeing other students in the hallway, sitting with friends at lunch, and working together in class makes the day more enjoyable. Sitting at a computer, alone at home, is boring and lonely.

5

Some people say that students can be successful in remote learning environments; however, I question their measure of success. While students may be able to learn some things on their own, everything would have been easier, faster, and more enjoyable if they had been in the classroom.

Annotation:

Transitional words of order introduce and organize reasons that support the claim.

Annotation:

Transitional words of contrast indicate opposing ideas in the counterclaim and the rebuttal.
