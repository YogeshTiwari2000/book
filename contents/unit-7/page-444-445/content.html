ENGAGE WITH WRITING

Analyze the Reading

A
Discuss the questions Search the text to find the evidence to support your rationale..
  1. Make inferences How do you think the author felt about the members of Congress when she delivered this speech? Support your rationale with evidence from the text.
    Discussion Starter: I can infer that the author . . . This rationale is supported by evidence in paragraph . . . when . . .
  2. Predict How do you think the author felt when the 19th Amendment was passed? Explain. Use evidence from the text.
  3. Make inferences How do you think most people in the United States felt about women’s suffrage in 1917? Support your rationale with evidence from the text.
B
Discuss the questions
  1. How did you feel about the author’s tone? Do you think this tone helped or hindered her argument?
  2. What was the most persuasive part of this speech in your viewpoint, and why?
    Discussion Starter: From my viewpoint, the most persuasive part of this speech was . . . because . . .
  3. Do you want to change your rating of the reading? Why or why not?
C
Make connections
  1. What connection does the author make between the fight for women’s suffrage and World War I?
  2. What connections to your own life did this reading lead to?
  3. Have you ever heard a persuasive speech about a different topic? Make a list of five possible topics for a persuasive speech.
D
Write Write a paragraph about another time in history or recently that people communicated their ideas for change.

Throughout history, people have used their voices to communicate new ideas and make things happen. This even occurred in our city when . . .

E
Revise Share your paragraph with a classmate and give each other suggestions for improvement. Revise your paragraph and incorporate the feedback.

Note It!

List the issues your classmates wrote about when people communicated their ideas for a change. Briefly note your viewpoint on each.
