Writing Skills Parentheses and Ellipses

Parentheses ( ) are punctuation marks used to set off:

  • words that define or explain another word

    Suffrage (or the right to vote) was not granted to American women until 1919.

  • added information or unrelated information

    Carrie Chapman Catt (from Iowa) was a famous suffragist.

  • an acronym

    Catt was a founder of the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA).

An ellipsis is punctuation (a set of three periods . . . ) used to indicate:

  • missing words or words removed from quoted material

    “Four score and seven years ago . . . that all men are created equal,” is the beginning of the Gettysburg Address.

  • a thought or line that trails off, often in a dialogue

    “I believe we need to require people to vote,” she said. As they walked off, I heard him say, “I don’t agree, I . . . “

Writing Skills Cohesion

Cohesion is when words and ideas link together effectively. To achieve cohesion, restate your ideas or refer to previous thoughts. You can do this by using referential words and phrases. For example, you can use:

  • pronouns (It, he, she, this, that . . . )

    The class president, Janine, was proud of her achievements. She discussed them in her speech.

  • nominalizations (the noun form of a verb or adjective)

    The new school policies differ from the previous ones. These differences are confusing.

    Students are more interested in the class this year. This interest is due to the new course book.

You can also create cohesion by using connecting words (transitional phrases and conjunctive adverbs) to link sentences.

Students voted for healthier lunches. In addition, they elected a class president who promised this. The principal, however, has not yet said if there is money for it.

Tip!

Gerunds

One way to make a noun form of a verb is to add -ing.
For example:
She runs every day. (Runs is a verb in this sentence.)

Running is her favorite activity.
(Running is a noun in this sentence.)
